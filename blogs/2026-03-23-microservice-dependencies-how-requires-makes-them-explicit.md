<article><p>Most microservice failures aren't an architecture problem — they're a visibility problem. This post maps several types of microservice coupling and shows how GraphQL Federation's @requires directive moves dependencies from hidden application code into the schema, where they're validated at build time and visible in query plans.</p><p>If two or more microservices share the same database, you don't have microservices. You have a distributed monolith.</p><p>That's not a controversial opinion anymore.</p><p>Twilio Segment famously <a href="https://www.twilio.com/en-us/blog/developers/best-practices/goodbye-microservices">walked away from 140 microservices</a> managed by 3 engineers. Many teams have publicly documented reverting from microservices to monoliths for small-to-medium applications.</p><p>The pendulum is swinging back, and the backlash is loud. But here's the thing: <strong>the problem was never microservices themselves. The problem was implicit dependencies.</strong></p><p>Most teams that &quot;failed at microservices&quot; failed because they moved from a monolith where all dependencies were visible to a distributed system where the dependencies became invisible. Hidden in HTTP calls buried in application code, shared libraries that create cascading update requirements, or databases that multiple services quietly read from.</p><p>The goal of microservices is team autonomy. But autonomy requires independence, and independence requires that your dependencies are <strong>explicit</strong>. If you can't see your dependencies, you can't manage them.</p><p>In this post, I'll introduce a taxonomy of microservice dependencies, explain why most architectures only address one or two types while ignoring the rest, and show how GraphQL Federation's <code>@requires</code> directive makes all of them explicit.</p><p>How are teams governing schema changes, handling production traffic, and measuring Federation success? Share your experience and get early access to the full report. For every valid survey completed, we'll donate $30 to <a href="https://www.unicef.org/">UNICEF</a>.</p><h2>The Taxonomy of Microservice Dependencies</h2><p>When engineers talk about microservice dependencies, they usually mean &quot;Service A calls Service B.&quot; But that's a massive oversimplification.</p><p>Sam Newman, in his book <a href="https://samnewman.io/books/building_microservices_2nd_edition/"><em>Building Microservices</em></a>, identifies four types of coupling. The broader industry adds several more. Here's the full taxonomy:</p><h3>1. Domain Coupling</h3><p>Service A interacts with Service B because of what B does. An Order service needs a Payment service to charge a card. This is unavoidable because services exist to do things for each other.</p><p>But is it <strong>visible</strong>?</p><h3>2. Data Coupling</h3><p>Service A needs <strong>specific data</strong> from Service B to compute something. A Shipping service needs the product weight from the Product service to calculate shipping costs. A Pricing service needs the user's subscription tier from the User service to apply discounts.</p><p>This is the coupling type most people think about first, and it's widely considered the hardest to solve. Christian Posta, VP at Solo.io and author of <em>Istio in Action</em>, argues that <em><a href="https://blog.christianposta.com/microservices/the-hardest-part-about-microservices-data/">the hardest part about microservices is your data.</a></em></p><h3>3. Temporal Coupling</h3><p>Service A needs Service B to be <strong>available right now</strong>. This happens whenever services communicate synchronously. If the Product service is down, the Shipping service can't calculate costs, even though all it needed was a weight field.</p><p>Temporal coupling is the #2 most discussed problem after data coupling, and it causes more production outages than any other type.</p><h3>4. Pass-Through Coupling</h3><p>Service A sends data through Service B to reach Service C. The Order service sends a shipping manifest through the Warehouse service to the Shipping service. Service B doesn't need the data, it just relays it.</p><p>This creates hidden dependencies. If the Shipping service changes its manifest format, both Service A and Service B need to change, even though Service B <em>never uses</em> the data.</p><h3>5. Common Coupling</h3><p>Multiple services share the same resource. A shared database, file system, or cache.</p><p>This is the classic &quot;distributed monolith&quot; smell. If two services read from the same database table, they are coupled through that table's schema. Change the schema, and both services break.</p><h3>6. Contract Coupling</h3><p>Service A depends on Service B's <strong>specific API interface</strong>. REST endpoint paths, request/response shapes, protobuf schemas. When B changes its API, A breaks.</p><p>This is well-understood, and tools like <a href="https://pact.io">Pact</a> and semantic versioning exist to manage it. But discipline is rare.</p><h3>7. Organizational Coupling</h3><p>Team boundaries don't match service boundaries. Team A owns a service but needs Team B to change another service first.</p><p>Martin Fowler and Sam Newman both argue this is often the <strong>root cause</strong> that manifests as all other types of coupling. <a href="https://martinfowler.com/bliki/ConwaysLaw.html">Conway's Law</a> isn't just an observation — it's a force of nature.</p><hr /><h2>The Real Problem: Implicit vs. Explicit</h2><p>Here's my central argument: <strong>every type of coupling above becomes manageable when it's explicit, and becomes toxic when it's implicit.</strong></p><p>Let's look at what &quot;implicit&quot; looks like in practice.</p><p>In a traditional microservice architecture, Service A needs the product weight to calculate shipping costs. So a developer writes an HTTP call:</p><pre>// Inside the Shipping service
async function calculateShippingCost(productId) {
  // Hidden dependency on Product service
  const product = await fetch(`http://product-service/products/${productId}`);
  const { weight } = await product.json();
  return weight * RATE_PER_KG;
}
</pre><p>This single piece of code creates <strong>three types of coupling at once</strong>:</p><ol><li><strong>Data coupling</strong> — we depend on the <code>weight</code> field from the Product service</li><li><strong>Temporal coupling</strong> — the Product service must be up right now</li><li><strong>Contract coupling</strong> — we depend on the exact REST endpoint path and response shape</li></ol><p>We’ve also hardcoded the service address, turning a logical dependency into a deployment constraint.</p><p>And none of it is visible outside this function. No architect looking at the system can see this dependency without reading every line of code in every service. No build system validates whether the Product service actually has a <code>weight</code> field. No query planner can optimize the call pattern.</p><p>Now imagine 50 services, each with dozens of these hidden calls.</p><p>That's not a microservice architecture. That's a distributed monolith with extra latency.</p><hr /><h2>So What Can We Do About It?</h2><p>We've established that the real problem is that most dependencies are <strong>implicit</strong>. Hidden in code, invisible at design time, and only discovered when something breaks.</p><p>What we need is an architecture that makes dependencies explicit by default. Not through discipline or documentation, but through the system itself.</p><p>That's exactly what GraphQL Federation does, or more specifically, the <code>@requires</code> directive.</p><h2>A Brief Primer on GraphQL Federation</h2><p>If you're already familiar with federation, feel free to skip ahead.</p><p>GraphQL Federation is a way to distribute a single GraphQL API across multiple services. Each service, called a <strong>subgraph</strong>, owns a portion of the overall schema. A <strong>router</strong> sits in front of all subgraphs, composes their schemas into a single unified API, and orchestrates requests across them.</p><p>The key building block is the <strong>entity</strong>. An entity is a type that can be uniquely identified and resolved across multiple subgraphs. You declare an entity by adding a <code>@key</code> directive:</p><pre>type Product @key(fields: &quot;id&quot;) {
  id: ID!
  name: String!
  weight: Float!
}
</pre><p>The <code>@key</code> tells the router: <em>&quot;You can look up a Product by its <code>id</code> from this subgraph.&quot;</em></p><p>Multiple subgraphs can contribute fields to the same entity. The router uses the key to stitch the data together. From the client's perspective, it looks like a single API.</p><p>This is what makes federation different from API gateways or schema stitching. The services don't know about each other. The router handles all the coordination, and the schema enforces consistency through composition checks at build time.</p><p>For a deeper dive, see <a href="https://wundergraph.com/blog/graphql-federation-history-entity-layer">The Evolution of GraphQL Federation and the Entity Layer</a>.</p><hr /><h2>How @requires Makes Dependencies Explicit</h2><p>With federation, services don't call each other. <strong>The router orchestrates all data fetching</strong>, and dependencies are declared in the schema using the <code>@requires</code> directive.</p><p>Here's the same shipping cost example:</p><pre># Product subgraph — owns product data
type Product @key(fields: &quot;id&quot;) {
  id: ID!
  name: String!
  weight: Float!
  price: Float!
}
</pre><pre># Shipping subgraph — depends on product weight
type Product @key(fields: &quot;id&quot;) {
  id: ID!
  weight: Float! @external
  shippingCost: Float! @requires(fields: &quot;weight&quot;)
}
</pre><p>That's it. The Shipping subgraph declares: <em>&quot;To resolve <code>shippingCost</code>, I need the <code>weight</code> field, which is owned by another subgraph.&quot;</em></p><p>The federation router handles the rest. When a client queries <code>shippingCost</code>, the router knows it must first fetch <code>weight</code> from the Product subgraph, then pass it to the Shipping subgraph's resolver.</p><p>Let's look at what changes:</p><table><thead><tr><th>Coupling Type</th><th>REST (Implicit)</th><th>Federation @requires (Explicit)</th></tr></thead><tbody><tr><td><strong>Domain</strong></td><td>Service A depends on Service B’s capabilities</td><td>Dependency is expressed through shared entities and fields in the schema</td></tr><tr><td><strong>Data</strong></td><td>Hidden in application code</td><td>Declared in the schema: <code>@requires(fields: &quot;weight&quot;)</code></td></tr><tr><td><strong>Temporal</strong></td><td>Shipping service must call Product service directly</td><td>Services don't call each other; the router still needs both subgraphs available, but the dependency is visible in query plans</td></tr><tr><td><strong>Pass-through</strong></td><td>Service B relays data it doesn't need</td><td>Router handles multi-hop resolution</td></tr><tr><td><strong>Common</strong></td><td>Temptation to share a database</td><td>Each subgraph owns its data; <code>@requires</code> bridges the gap</td></tr><tr><td><strong>Contract</strong></td><td>Depends on REST endpoint paths and response shapes</td><td>Depends on specific GraphQL fields, validated at composition time</td></tr><tr><td><strong>Organizational</strong></td><td>Hidden dependencies require back-channel coordination</td><td>Schema makes dependencies visible to all teams</td></tr></tbody></table><p>To be clear: <code>@requires</code> doesn't make temporal coupling disappear. The router still needs the Product subgraph to be available to fetch the <code>weight</code> field. But the dependency is no longer hidden in a function body somewhere. It's in the schema, the query plan, and it's validated at build time.</p><p><strong>The dependency still exists, but now you can see it, plan for it, and manage it.</strong></p><hr /><h2>The Power of Explicit: What You Can See, You Can Manage</h2><p>Once dependencies are in the schema, you unlock capabilities that are simply impossible with implicit dependencies.</p><h3>Dependency Graphs at Design Time</h3><p>With <code>@requires</code>, every cross-service dependency is a first-class citizen of the schema. You can compute a full dependency graph without running a single request. Which subgraph depends on which fields? Which fields are the most &quot;depended upon&quot; across the entire federated graph?</p><p>If all your subgraphs depend on a single service through <code>@requires</code>, you can see that immediately. That service is a bottleneck, and everyone should pay extra attention to its reliability.</p><p>With implicit HTTP calls, you'd need to analyze runtime traces, logs, and metrics to get the same picture. Even then, you'd only see the dependencies that actually fired during your observation window.</p><h3>Query Plans Show Exactly What Happens</h3><p>Every query in a federated graph produces a query plan. The query plan shows exactly which subgraphs will be called, in what order, and which fields are fetched at each step.</p><p>When you add a <code>@requires</code> dependency to a field, you can immediately see how query plans change. You can preview the performance impact <strong>before deploying anything</strong>.</p><p>Compare this to adding a new HTTP call in a REST service. How do you know the impact? You have to deploy it, watch the metrics, and hope nothing breaks. With explicit field dependencies, you see all of this at design time.</p><h3>Composition Checks Validate Dependencies at Build Time</h3><p>When you publish a subgraph schema that uses <code>@requires</code>, the composition engine validates that the required fields actually exist, that they're correctly marked as <code>@external</code>, and that the dependency graph is resolvable.</p><p>If someone removes a field that another subgraph requires, <strong>the build fails</strong>. Not the runtime. Not a production incident. The build.</p><p>Compare this to REST APIs, where you might deprecate an endpoint or change a response shape, and the only way to know if something breaks is to deploy and find out. With federation, you simply can't break the contract. Composition checks enforce it.</p><p>This is the equivalent of a compiler catching a type error. Except instead of a single codebase, you're validating contracts across an entire distributed system.</p><p>With tools like <a href="https://wundergraph.com/blog/missing-layer-in-graphql-federation">WunderGraph Hub</a>, you can see these dependencies at design time, preview how query plans change when you add or modify a <code>@requires</code> dependency, and catch breaking changes before they ever reach production. The dependency graph isn't just explicit, it's actively guarded.</p><hr /><h2>Field-Level Precision: Not Endpoints, Fields</h2><p>There's a subtlety in <code>@requires</code> that's easy to overlook but incredibly important.</p><p>With REST APIs, you depend on <strong>entire endpoints</strong>. Your service calls <code>GET /products/{id}</code> and gets back the full product object, even if you only need the weight. The Product service has no idea which fields you actually use.</p><p>With <code>@requires(fields: &quot;weight&quot;)</code>, the dependency is at the <strong>field level</strong>. The Product subgraph knows exactly which fields other subgraphs depend on. Not which endpoints. Not which objects. Which <strong>specific fields</strong>.</p><p>This precision has real consequences:</p><ul><li><strong>The Product team</strong> knows that <code>weight</code> is a critical field because 3 other subgraphs require it. They'll think twice before changing its type or removing it.</li><li><strong>The Shipping team</strong> knows they depend on exactly one field from one subgraph, not an opaque REST endpoint that might change.</li><li><strong>The platform team</strong> can identify the &quot;hotspot&quot; fields that serve as edges between services and monitor them accordingly.</li></ul><p>If we combine the fields most used as &quot;edges&quot; between two services, we know exactly where the bottlenecks and coupling hotspots are. This is information that simply doesn't exist in an implicit architecture.</p><hr /><h2>A Note on @provides</h2><p>I want to be transparent about <code>@provides</code> because I see it discussed as a companion to <code>@requires</code>, but I think the comparison is misleading.</p><p><code>@provides</code> declares that a resolver can return fields that are owned by another subgraph. It's an optimization hint for the router: <em>&quot;When you call this field, I can also give you these other fields cheaply, so you don't need to make another fetch.&quot;</em></p><pre>type Query {
  topProducts: [Product!]! @provides(fields: &quot;name description&quot;)
}

type Product @key(fields: &quot;id&quot;) {
  id: ID!
  name: String! @external
  description: String! @external
}
</pre><p>This can be useful in specific cases, but it comes with a tradeoff that's rarely discussed: <code>@provides</code> makes fields <strong>shareable</strong>, meaning they can be resolved from multiple subgraphs.</p><p>In many cases, this is an anti-pattern. If two subgraphs can resolve the same field with the same data, the data needs to live somewhere accessible to both. And if the data is the same, <code>@provides</code> might be an indication that the data source is shared.</p><p>That's not always the case, but it's a smell worth investigating.</p><p>My recommendation: <strong>use <code>@requires</code> for expressing dependencies. Use <code>@provides</code> sparingly, and only when you're certain the data isn't shared from a common source.</strong></p><hr /><h2>Service Boundaries: The Root Cause Most Teams Miss</h2><p>Before reaching for any technical solution, there's a more fundamental question most teams get wrong: <strong>where do you draw the service boundary?</strong></p><p>The most common mistake I see is creating too many services. Teams split along technical layers (a database service, an auth service, a notification service) instead of along team boundaries.</p><p>My rule is simple: <strong>a team should be able to ship a product change by modifying a single service.</strong></p><p>If a product change requires coordinated changes across multiple services, your services are too small. Code that changes together should live in the same subgraph.</p><p>If multiple teams must coordinate to make a single product change, your service ownership is spread too wide.</p><p>The goal is team autonomy. Autonomy brings velocity. A good starting point is one service per team, although a team can own multiple services when they are truly independent — for example, when the code never changes together in the same PR.</p><p>Once your boundaries are right, federation's <code>@requires</code> makes the remaining inter-service dependencies explicit, manageable, and visible. But no amount of tooling will fix bad boundaries.</p><hr /><h2>The Industry Is Stuck on Implicit</h2><p>Let me tie this back to industry trends we see.</p><p>Companies are reverting to monoliths, not because microservices are fundamentally flawed, but because the tooling for managing implicit dependencies never materialized.</p><p>The alternatives people reach for:</p><ul><li><strong>Event-driven choreography</strong> — reduces temporal coupling but introduces eventual consistency, event ordering complexity, and makes the dependency graph even more implicit</li><li><strong>API gateways</strong> — manage contract coupling but do nothing for data, temporal, or organizational coupling</li><li><strong>Contract testing tools like Pact</strong> — useful but low adoption, and they only catch contract coupling</li><li><strong>&quot;Just be more disciplined&quot;</strong> — not actionable at scale</li></ul><p>The gap is clear: <strong>There is no widely adopted solution that makes all types of microservice dependencies explicit and manageable at design time.</strong></p><p>Except for federation with <code>@requires</code>.</p><p>The schema becomes the single source of truth for your dependency graph. Composition checks validate it at build time. Query plans make it visible at design time. Field-level precision tells each team exactly who depends on what.</p><p>This isn't a new problem. But it's a problem that finally has an elegant solution.</p><hr /><h2>Conclusion</h2><p>Microservice dependencies are not a single problem. They're several problems: domain, data, temporal, pass-through, common, contract, and organizational coupling.</p><p>Most architectures address one or two. The rest stay implicit, hidden in application code, and slowly turn your microservices into a distributed monolith.</p><p>GraphQL Federation's <code>@requires</code> directive addresses most of them simultaneously through one mechanism: <strong>making dependencies explicit and moving orchestration to the router.</strong></p><p>What you can see, you can manage. What you can't see will eventually page you at 3 AM.</p><p>Make your dependencies explicit. Your future self will thank you.</p><p>If you want to see what explicit dependency management looks like in practice — dependency graphs, query plan previews, and composition checks that catch breaking changes before they reach production — take a look at <a href="https://wundergraph.com/hub">WunderGraph Hub</a>.</p><hr /><hr /></article>
